India-ASEAN Free Trade Agreement Review: A Critical Juncture

Negotiations enter a critical phase as India seeks improved market access for services while ASEAN pushes for tariff concessions on goods.

By The Governance Gazette Desk  ·  April 13, 2026  ·  7 min read

Illustration: The Governance Gazette

The review of the India-ASEAN Free Trade Agreement in Goods (AITIGA), which has been ongoing since 2022, has entered a critical phase. The original agreement, signed in 2009 and implemented in 2010, has been criticised by Indian industry for creating a trade deficit that has grown to over $43 billion — largely favouring ASEAN nations.

Background: What is AITIGA?

The ASEAN-India Trade in Goods Agreement (AITIGA) is a free trade agreement that provides preferential tariff access for goods traded between India and the 10 ASEAN member states — Brunei, Cambodia, Indonesia, Laos, Malaysia, Myanmar, Philippines, Singapore, Thailand, and Vietnam. Under the agreement, India agreed to eliminate or reduce tariffs on about 80% of traded goods.

India's Core Concerns

India's primary concern is that the agreement has been used as a backdoor route for Chinese goods — particularly electronics and steel — to enter India through ASEAN countries with minimal value addition but availing of FTA tariff benefits. This has contributed to India's widening trade deficit with ASEAN.

India is also pushing for a comprehensive review of the Rules of Origin provisions — the criteria that determine whether a product "originates" from an ASEAN country and is therefore eligible for FTA benefits.

What India Wants

India wants a separate and comprehensive Services FTA with ASEAN, which would give Indian IT professionals, nurses, and other service providers easier market access across ASEAN economies. The current agreement covers goods only, and India's competitive advantage lies strongly in services.
