India's Current Account Deficit Narrows to 0.9% of GDP: What It Means

Services exports and remittance inflows cushion the trade deficit as merchandise exports remain under pressure from global demand slowdown.

By The Governance Gazette Desk  ·  April 13, 2026  ·  7 min read

Illustration: The Governance Gazette

India's current account deficit (CAD) narrowed significantly to 0.9% of GDP in the third quarter of the current fiscal year, down from 1.8% in the same period last year, according to data released by the Reserve Bank of India. The improvement was driven by a surge in services exports — particularly software and IT-enabled services — and robust remittance inflows from the Indian diaspora.

What is the Current Account?

The current account is one of the two primary components of a country's Balance of Payments (BoP), the other being the capital account. The current account records all transactions involving goods, services, income, and current transfers between a country and the rest of the world over a specific period.

A Current Account Deficit means India is importing more goods and services (and paying more income abroad) than it is exporting and earning. A deficit must be financed by capital inflows — foreign direct investment (FDI), foreign portfolio investment (FPI), external commercial borrowings, or drawdown of foreign exchange reserves.

Why Has the CAD Narrowed?

Several factors explain the improvement. Software services exports have grown 12% year-on-year, making India one of the world's largest services exporters. Remittances from the Indian diaspora — the largest in the world — have remained robust at over $100 billion annually. Meanwhile, crude oil prices have moderated, reducing India's import bill significantly since petroleum products constitute about 30% of total imports.

Why Does CAD Matter?

A high CAD puts pressure on the Indian rupee, as more dollars are flowing out than coming in. RBI often has to intervene in forex markets to prevent sharp rupee depreciation. When CAD is financed by volatile FPI flows rather than stable FDI, it creates vulnerability to sudden capital outflows during global risk-off events.
