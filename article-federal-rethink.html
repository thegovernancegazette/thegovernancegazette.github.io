Why India's Federal Structure Needs a Fundamental Rethink

Cooperative federalism cannot survive when the Centre holds all fiscal cards and states are left bargaining at the table.

By The Governance Gazette Desk  ·  April 13, 2026  ·  7 min read

Illustration: The Governance Gazette

The idea of cooperative federalism — states and Centre working as equal partners toward shared national goals — has become the defining aspiration of India's intergovernmental relations in the 21st century. Prime Ministers invoke it in speeches, Finance Commissions build it into their frameworks, and the GST Council is held up as its institutional embodiment.

And yet, the reality on the ground tells a different story. Southern states are fighting the Finance Commission's devolution formula. Governors are acting as partisan instruments of the Centre in non-BJP states. The GST Council's consensus model is creaking under pressure from revenue disputes. The Inter-State Council, designed to be the premier forum for Centre-state dialogue, has met only twelve times since 1990.

The Fiscal Asymmetry Problem

The most fundamental problem with Indian federalism today is fiscal asymmetry. States are responsible for nearly 60% of total government expenditure — on education, health, agriculture, and rural development — but control only about 40% of total revenues. This mismatch forces states into perpetual dependence on central transfers, grants, and loans.

The 15th Finance Commission did attempt to correct this through performance-linked grants and a somewhat higher devolution share, but the fundamental architecture remains: the Centre collects, the states spend, and the Finance Commission is the intermediary whose recommendations the Centre is not legally bound to accept in their entirety.

The Way Forward

Genuine federalism requires three reforms: a constitutionally mandated minimum devolution share that cannot be altered by executive action, an empowered and regularly convened Inter-State Council with binding dispute resolution mechanisms, and depoliticised Governors appointed through a consultative process involving the state government.

Without these structural changes, cooperative federalism will remain what it largely is today — a rhetorical aspiration that papers over a centralised reality.
