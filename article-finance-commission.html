15th Finance Commission's Devolution Formula Sparks Fresh Debate Among Southern States

Four states formally write to the Union Finance Ministry seeking a review of the vertical devolution ratio, citing demographic performance penalties.

By The Governance Gazette Desk  ·  April 13, 2026  ·  7 min read

The Parliament of India — where Finance Commission recommendations are tabled each session.

The ongoing tussle between the Union government and southern states over horizontal tax devolution has intensified significantly, with Tamil Nadu, Kerala, Karnataka and Telangana jointly submitting a memorandum to the Union Finance Ministry seeking reconsideration of population-based weightage criteria that they argue penalises states for achieving better demographic outcomes.

What is the Finance Commission?

The Finance Commission is a constitutional body established under Article 280 of the Indian Constitution. It is constituted every five years by the President of India and is tasked with recommending the distribution of tax revenues between the Union and the states, and among the states themselves.

The 15th Finance Commission, chaired by N.K. Singh, submitted its final report covering the period 2021–26. It recommended a vertical devolution of 41% of the divisible pool of central taxes to states — a marginal reduction from the 14th Commission's 42%, primarily to account for the newly created Union Territories of Jammu & Kashmir and Ladakh.

What is the Southern States' Objection?

The controversy centres on the horizontal devolution formula — which determines how the states' collective share is distributed among individual states. The formula uses multiple criteria including population (based on 2011 Census data), area, forest and ecology, income distance, demographic performance, and tax effort.

Southern states argue that the population criterion structurally disadvantages them because they controlled population growth successfully in the 1970s–90s, but the formula uses 2011 Census data, rewarding states with higher populations — which are largely northern states that did not achieve similar demographic transitions.

"You are penalising states that followed national family planning policy faithfully. This is a perverse incentive built into the federal fiscal architecture." — Tamil Nadu Finance Minister

The Demographic Dividend Paradox

This is a central tension in India's cooperative federalism. Southern states — Tamil Nadu, Kerala, Karnataka, Andhra Pradesh and Telangana — have Total Fertility Rates (TFRs) well below the replacement level of 2.1. Northern states like Uttar Pradesh and Bihar still have TFRs above 3.0.

Because Lok Sabha seat allocation and Finance Commission weights are both influenced by population, southern states face a double disadvantage: fewer seats in Parliament and lower tax devolution shares.

What Changes Are Being Sought?

The joint memorandum submitted by the four states asks for: replacement of 2011 Census data with 1971 Census data for population-based weightage (as was done before the 14th Commission), increase in the weightage given to demographic performance from the current 12.5%, and a floor guarantee ensuring no state's devolution share falls below a minimum threshold.

UPSC Civil Services Relevance

  • GS2 Paper: Functions and responsibilities of the Union and the States; Finance Commission – role and recent functioning; Issues and challenges pertaining to the federal structure
  • Key Articles: Article 280 (Finance Commission), Article 270 (Taxes levied and distributed between Union and States), Article 275 (Grants from the Union to certain States)
  • Probable Mains Question (250 words): "The horizontal devolution formula of the Finance Commission has become a source of friction in Centre-state relations. Examine the issues and suggest reforms to make the formula more equitable." (GS2)
  • Keywords to use: Cooperative federalism, fiscal federalism, vertical devolution, horizontal devolution, divisible pool, demographic transition, TFR

The 16th Finance Commission, which will cover 2026–31, is expected to be constituted soon. The outcome of these representations will significantly shape how India's fiscal federalism evolves over the next decade.
