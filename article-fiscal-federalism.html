Fiscal Federalism in India: Every Concept an Aspirant Must Master

Finance Commission, FRBM Act, vertical and horizontal devolution, grants-in-aid — a complete conceptual map with PYQ linkages from 2018 to 2024.

By The Governance Gazette Desk  ·  April 13, 2026  ·  7 min read

Illustration: The Governance Gazette

Fiscal federalism is one of the most consistently tested topics in UPSC Civil Services Mains — appearing in GS2 (Centre-state relations), GS3 (Indian economy), and even in Essay papers. This guide maps every concept you need, with PYQ linkages and answer framework hints.

What is Fiscal Federalism?

Fiscal federalism refers to the division of financial powers and responsibilities between the Centre and the states in a federal system. In India, this is governed by Articles 264–293 of the Constitution (Part XII) and administered primarily through the Finance Commission.

Vertical vs Horizontal Devolution

Vertical devolution refers to the share of central taxes that goes to states collectively — currently 41% of the divisible pool as recommended by the 15th Finance Commission. Horizontal devolution refers to how this 41% is distributed among individual states, based on criteria like population, area, income distance, demographic performance, and tax effort.

The Finance Commission (Article 280)

Constituted every 5 years by the President, the Finance Commission recommends: the share of central taxes going to states (vertical devolution), the distribution formula among states (horizontal devolution), principles governing grants-in-aid to states under Article 275, and any other matter referred by the President.

Grants-in-Aid (Article 275)

Beyond tax devolution, the Constitution allows Parliament to give grants-in-aid to states in need of assistance. These are different from shared taxes and are used for specific purposes like tribal area development, performance incentives, and disaster management.

FRBM Act, 2003

The Fiscal Responsibility and Budget Management Act mandates both Centre and states to maintain fiscal discipline — controlling fiscal deficit, revenue deficit, and debt levels. States have their own FRBM Acts. The NK Singh Committee (2017) recommended a debt-GDP ratio target of 60% (40% Centre + 20% states).

PYQ Linkages (2018–2024)

  • 2024: "The Finance Commission of India has been increasingly called upon to perform non-traditional functions. Examine." (GS2)
  • 2022: "Critically examine the role of the Finance Commission in furthering cooperative federalism in India." (GS2)
  • 2019: "How far do you think cooperation, competition and confrontation have shaped the nature of federation in India?" (GS2)
  • 2018: "The concept of cooperative federalism has been increasingly stressed in recent years. Highlight the drawbacks in the existing structure and the extent to which cooperative federalism would answer the shortcomings." (GS2)
