Draft National Forest Policy 2026: Five Key Changes Every Citizen Must Know

The revised policy introduces community forest management boards and strengthens gram sabha rights over forest land diversion decisions.

By The Governance Gazette Desk  ·  April 13, 2026  ·  7 min read

Illustration: The Governance Gazette

The Ministry of Environment, Forest and Climate Change has released the Draft National Forest Policy 2026, replacing the landmark 1988 policy that has governed India's forest management for over three decades. The new draft reflects India's commitments under the Paris Agreement, the Kunming-Montreal Global Biodiversity Framework, and the evolving jurisprudence around tribal and forest rights.

The Five Key Changes

1. Community Forest Management Boards

The draft introduces statutory Community Forest Management Boards at the district level, giving gram sabhas a formal institutional role in forest governance decisions. This builds on the Forest Rights Act 2006 and addresses the long-standing gap between the law and its implementation on the ground.

2. Carbon Market Linkage

For the first time, the policy explicitly links forest conservation to India's carbon credit market, allowing forest-rich states to monetise conservation efforts. States like Chhattisgarh, Odisha, and Jharkhand — which have historically lost revenue by maintaining large forest covers — stand to benefit significantly from this provision.

3. Urban Forest Mandate

Cities with a population over 10 lakh are mandated to maintain a minimum green cover of 20% of their municipal area. This is the first time a national forest policy has explicitly addressed urban forestry, reflecting the growing recognition of urban heat islands and the health benefits of tree cover in cities.

4. Agroforestry Targets

The policy sets an explicit target of bringing 10% of agricultural land under agroforestry by 2030. Agroforestry — integrating trees into farming systems — is seen as a key strategy for achieving India's NDC targets while simultaneously increasing farmer incomes.

5. Tribal Rights Strengthened

The draft strengthens the implementation of the Forest Rights Act 2006 by making gram sabha consent mandatory for any diversion of forest land for non-forest purposes. This addresses a significant governance gap where forest diversion was proceeding without adequate community consent.

UPSC Civil Services Relevance

  • GS3 Paper: Conservation, environmental pollution and degradation; Forest Rights Act; Biodiversity; Climate change
  • GS1 Paper: Distribution of key natural resources across the world including South Asia and the Indian sub-continent
  • Probable Mains Question: "The Draft National Forest Policy 2026 attempts to balance conservation with community rights. Critically examine its key provisions." (GS3, 250 words)
  • Key Laws to link: Forest Rights Act 2006, Forest Conservation Act 1980, Wildlife Protection Act 1972, Environment Protection Act 1986
