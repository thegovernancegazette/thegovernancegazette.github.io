Model Answer: 'Critically Examine India's Federal Structure' — 250 Words

A structured model answer with introduction, body, conclusion, examiner keywords, and notes from a 2025 AIR 47 qualifier.

By The Governance Gazette Desk  ·  April 13, 2026  ·  7 min read

Illustration: The Governance Gazette

The Question

"India is often described as a 'quasi-federal' state with a unitary bias. Critically examine India's federal structure in light of recent constitutional and political developments." (GS2, 2024 pattern, 250 words)

Model Answer

Introduction: India's Constitution establishes a federal structure with a strong unitary bias — described by K.C. Wheare as "quasi-federal." While it distributes legislative, executive, and financial powers between the Centre and states, several provisions enable central predominance.

Federal Features: Written constitution with supremacy, dual polity (Centre and states), bicameral legislature, independent judiciary, and separate domains in the Seventh Schedule's three lists demonstrate federal character. The 73rd and 74th Amendments further devolved powers to local bodies.

Unitary Bias: However, Article 356 (President's Rule), Article 249 (Parliament legislating on State List subjects in national interest), appointment of Governors by the Centre, All India Services under central control, and the Concurrent List's predominance in favour of Centre reflect unitary tendencies. Recent judicial scrutiny of Governor's conduct — in Tamil Nadu, Kerala, and West Bengal — highlights how the office is being used as a political instrument.

Critical Assessment: The Finance Commission's devolution formula has become a source of friction, particularly for southern states penalised for demographic performance. The GST Council, though a cooperative mechanism, has exposed limitations in revenue autonomy.

Conclusion: India needs to evolve from cooperative to collaborative federalism — where states are genuine partners in national policymaking, not recipients of central directives. Reforming the Finance Commission's criteria, depoliticising Governors, and strengthening the Inter-State Council are essential steps.

Examiner's Notes — AIR 47, 2025

  • What worked: Clear structure, used specific article numbers, contemporary examples (Governor controversy), mentioned reform suggestions
  • Keywords that scored: quasi-federal, unitary bias, cooperative federalism, collaborative federalism, Seventh Schedule, Inter-State Council
  • What to avoid: Listing features without critical analysis; not connecting to recent events; generic conclusion without specific reforms
  • Ideal word count: 240–260 words. Never exceed 280 for a 250-word question.
